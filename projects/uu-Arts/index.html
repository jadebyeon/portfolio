University Unions Arts Initiative

Designing Systems for Campus Arts Engagement

UX & Systems Designer · May–July 2025 · University of Michigan

Role: Research, IA, Service Design Focus: Submission flow, gallery mapping, workflows

Design Problem

University Unions offered a variety of gallery and display spaces for student and faculty artwork, but there was no centralized digital infrastructure for artists to understand where, how, and when they could exhibit. The submission process was fragmented, space details were not visible online, and institutional coordination (e.g., contracts, installation workflows) lacked clear documentation.

As the Arts Initiative Coordinator, I set out to design a system that improves human–computer interaction across multiple user types — student artists, staff curators, and community partners — by building tools that enable clarity, accessibility, and institutional memory.

Research & Strategy

To frame the design approach, I conducted:

  • Heuristic evaluation of current submission flow and web architecture
  • Stakeholder interviews with CMD, Facilities, Art Curators, and past artists
  • Site audits of display areas across the Union, League, and Pierpont
  • Review of peer institutions’ gallery tools (e.g., UMMA, Yale Center for British Art, STAMPS Gallery)

These insights revealed three strategic priorities:

Clarify the submission experience through guided user flows
Visualize physical spaces to improve pre-installation planning
Create modular infrastructure for documentation, signage, and rotation strategy

System Development

Webpage & Submission System

  • Prototyped a dedicated “Arts at the Unions” webpage for the uunions.umich.edu site
  • Designed responsive sections: About, Submission Form, Exhibit Calendar, Gallery Map, Artist Resources
  • Rebuilt the Google Form intake process with conditional logic for internal vs. external users
  • Integrated copy and layout suggestions based on institutional voice

Display Strategy & Mapping

  • Produced a visual map of available art spaces, annotated with dimensions, lighting, and seasonal considerations
  • Created a proposal for signage, display banners, and rotational curation of works (e.g., BHM, Women’s History Month)
  • Initiated documentation of art locations and archived photos for future intake planning

Workflow Infrastructure

  • Coordinated department-specific SignNow setup for digital art loan agreements
  • Created internal guides on contracting, installation, and archival steps
  • Assisted in live exhibition installs (e.g., IHP: Visual History of the Unions, Redefining the Crown)

Deliverables

Webpage Prototype (Adobe XD) Submission System Flow (Google Forms) University Unions Display Area Guide

Reflection

This project taught me that designing for engagement in institutional environments often requires more than user-friendly interfaces — it demands system-level thinking that connects digital tools to physical space, administrative processes, and organizational culture.

One area I would improve next time is stakeholder onboarding earlier in the design process. Because the initiative was relatively new, some of the ideas (like webpage integration or signage redesign) required more rounds of clarification and alignment than I anticipated. In the future, I would develop co-design workshops or structured feedback checkpoints earlier, so that collaborators from marketing, facilities, and programming could help shape and own the system from the outset.

This experience also reinforced that designing scalable systems — even for something as analog as a gallery wall — has deep cross-context value. The methods I used here (process mapping, conditional logic for form flows, visual documentation) could easily translate to community archives, nonprofit event workflows, or digital tools for public engagement. Whether in design research, arts strategy, or civic tech, I now feel more equipped to build infrastructure that enables creative and operational clarity across users, teams, and environments.
